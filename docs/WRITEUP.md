# Building a real-time, explainable fraud detector — and the engineering study behind it

*An independent reproduction of a 2025 fraud-detection paper, turned into a deployed, explainable,
monitored system — with an argument to defend and the honest caveats to go with it.*

**Live demo:** https://huggingface.co/spaces/Leonardasvekrikas-source/fraud-detection-demo
· **Code:** https://github.com/Leonardasvekrikas-source/fraud-detection-system

---

## Why this project exists

Most machine-learning portfolios stop at a notebook: load a dataset, train a model, print an
F1 score. That proves you can *fit* a model. It doesn't prove you can *ship* one — reproduce
research faithfully, deploy it, explain its decisions, watch it for decay, and be honest about
what works and what doesn't.

My MSc thesis built a hybrid LightGBM–LSTM fraud detector with explainability and a comparison of
model-fusion strategies. This project turns that research into a **production-grade system with an
argument**:

> A **decision-level** fusion of LightGBM and LSTM — keeping the two models separate and combining
> their scores — is a more practical production choice than **feature-level** fusion for this
> problem. It is simpler, lower-latency, and stays explainable (the models remain separable, so a
> fast, exact SHAP `TreeExplainer` can attribute the score), and it adds a human-in-the-loop
> *Expert-Checking* tier for ambiguous cases.

Everything below is evidence for — and honest scrutiny of — that claim. The method itself comes
from the literature (Yousefimehr & Ghatee, 2025, *Expert Systems with Applications* 262, 125661);
the reimplementation, the experiments, the serving system, the monitoring, and the conclusions are
mine.

## 1. Reproduce the research first (be able to prove your numbers)

Before building anything on top, I reimplemented the paper's dual-subsystem pipeline as a tested
Python package (`src/` layout, config-driven, no notebooks in the pipeline) and reproduced its
results on the European Credit Card Fraud dataset:

| Metric | LightGBM (mine) | LightGBM (paper) | LSTM (mine) | LSTM (paper) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | **0.8503 ± 0.012** | 0.8805 | 0.7723 ± 0.058 | 0.8310 |
| AUC | **0.9851 ± 0.005** | 0.9603 | 0.9525 ± 0.025 | 0.8922 |

The LightGBM branch reproduces the paper **bit-for-bit** (deterministic, fixed seed); the LSTM
tracks it within run-to-run noise (neural training isn't bit-reproducible across TensorFlow
versions). Crucially, these numbers are **regenerated by the code**, not quoted — anyone can run
one command and get them.

I also documented a real limitation I hit: the `HybridUS` undersampler has an edge case under
extreme imbalance where distribution-protected samples exceed the undersampling budget, so the
LSTM trains near the original imbalance. That's the paper's behavior, not a bug — but naming it is
the difference between a reproduction and a reproduction you understand.

## 2. The engineering study (the argument, made legible)

With a trustworthy baseline, I ran four tracked experiments (MLflow), each answering a question.

### Feature-level vs decision-level fusion

I trained both base models, extracted LightGBM leaf embeddings (PCA→50) and LSTM hidden states,
concatenated them, and trained an MLP meta-classifier (feature-level fusion) — then compared all
four configurations on the same test split:

| Configuration | Precision | Recall | F1 | AUC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LightGBM | 0.963 | 0.703 | 0.813 | 0.964 |
| LSTM | 1.000 | 0.676 | 0.806 | 0.972 |
| **Decision-level fusion** | 1.000 | 0.608 | 0.756 | 0.972 |
| **Feature-level fusion** | **0.032** | 0.905 | **0.062** | 0.968 |

The result is a **negative result that supports the argument**: feature-level fusion keeps
competitive *ranking* (AUC 0.968) but its precision and F1 **collapse** — the meta-classifier's
decision boundary is miscalibrated by the extreme class imbalance in its training data. The
simpler decision-level approach stays clean with far less machinery.

**Honesty note I insist on:** on raw detection score, **LightGBM alone is actually the strongest
single model here.** Decision-level fusion's value isn't a higher F1 — it's competitive ranking
*plus* simplicity, per-model attributability for explanations, and the Expert-Checking escalation.
I don't claim more than the data shows.

### The threshold is a cost decision, not a curve

A fraud model's threshold is a business lever: a missed fraud (false negative) costs the fraud
loss; a false alarm (false positive) costs review friction. I swept the threshold across a range
of cost ratios `R = cost(FN)/cost(FP)` and found the cost-minimizing operating point for each.

As a miss gets costlier, the optimal threshold drops (0.21 → 0.002) and recall climbs — but it
**plateaus around 0.79**: a residual set of frauds is scored near-zero by LightGBM and can't be
recovered by *any* threshold. That ceiling is exactly the case for the sequential (LSTM) signal
and the human-review tier — threshold tuning alone won't catch them.

### Are the scores trustworthy? (calibration)

Fraud teams act on *scores*, not just labels — so a predicted 0.8 should mean ~80% of such
transactions really are fraud. My model is trained on resampled data (~5% fraud) vs a true base
rate of ~0.17%, which inflates its probabilities. The subtle part: the *aggregate* calibration
error is a near-zero, **misleading** 0.0004 — the flood of easy negatives (all scored ≈0) swamps
it. Measured **in the region the model actually flags**, the raw scores are 7.5% miscalibrated;
post-hoc isotonic calibration (fit at the true base rate) cuts that ~3.7× to 2%. The lesson —
that aggregate calibration metrics lie under extreme imbalance — is the kind of thing you only
learn by looking.

### Does it generalize? (PaySim)

I ran the pipeline on the structurally different PaySim dataset (interpretable mobile-money
features instead of PCA components). LightGBM **transferred strongly** — AUC 0.992, recall 0.706,
essentially matching the thesis. The LSTM ranked well (AUC 0.96) but under-fired: PaySim fraud is
more concentrated and *less sequential*, so the tabular branch carries the signal.

This was also where I caught my own methodology bug. My first attempt subsampled PaySim's training
data *randomly* for tractability — which **shredded the temporal ordering the LSTM depends on**,
giving a meaningless AUC of 0.48. I fixed it to use a *contiguous* tail block (sequences intact)
and split the data budget so LightGBM uses the full train while only the LSTM subsamples. Catching
and fixing that — rather than shipping a misleading number — is the point.

## 3. Ship it (real-time, explainable)

A FastAPI service ([live](https://huggingface.co/spaces/Leonardasvekrikas-source/fraud-detection-demo))
scores a transaction and returns a fraud probability, a verdict (Normal / Fraud / Expert-Checking),
and a **SHAP explanation** of the top contributing features. Because the models stay separable, the
LightGBM component gets an **exact, fast `TreeExplainer`** — the explainability payoff of the
decision-level design.

The explanations are faithful to the research: on a real fraud transaction, the live endpoint
returns `V14, V12, V10, V4` as the top drivers — exactly the features the thesis's SHAP analysis
identified. It's Dockerized (a lean ~1.5 GB image) and deployed to Hugging Face Spaces.

Building this surfaced the kind of bug that only shows on a clean environment: a `.gitignore` rule
meant for the trained-model *output* directory (`artifacts/`) was **silently swallowing a source
package** of the same name (`src/fraud_detection/artifacts/`). It worked on my machine (the files
existed locally) but was missing from every fresh clone — CI, Docker, and the deploy. The lesson:
`git ls-files`, not just local tests, tells you what's actually in your repo.

## 4. Keep it honest in production (drift + retraining)

A model trained on a snapshot degrades *silently* as data drifts. Two pieces close that loop:

- **Drift monitoring (Evidently).** Compares a reference distribution against a current batch and
  flags drifted features/target via distance tests. I validated it *discriminates*: zero false
  alarms on a stable batch, and it pinpoints exactly the features I perturbed in a simulated drift
  scenario (clearly labeled simulated — this is offline public data, not a live feed).

- **Retraining DAG (Airflow, local docker-compose).** A champion/challenger loop —
  `ingest → train_candidate → evaluate_candidate → promote_if_better` — that promotes a freshly
  trained model **only if it beats the live one on a holdout**. The decision logic lives in the
  *tested* package; the DAG only orchestrates (so the important part is unit-tested, not buried in
  Airflow). I validated it end-to-end: the image builds, the DAG parses, and `airflow dags test`
  runs the whole loop, trains a model, and promotes it.

Deliberately **not** a 24/7 server or a Kubernetes cluster — a local, honest, illustrative pipeline.
Over-engineering would undercut the point.

## What's mine vs. the literature

- **From the paper (Yousefimehr & Ghatee, 2025):** the hybrid method — LightGBM + LSTM dual
  subsystems, HybridOS/HybridUS distribution-preserving resampling, F2Vote feature selection, the
  Algorithm-1 decision-level fusion, and the SHAP/LIME pairing.
- **Mine:** the independent, tested reimplementation; the fusion experiment and its negative result;
  the cost-as-threshold analysis; the PaySim generalization test; the serving system + live demo;
  the drift monitoring and retraining pipeline; the CI; and every engineering conclusion here.

## What I'd tell an interviewer it taught me

- Reproducing research faithfully is a skill in itself — and being able to *regenerate* the numbers
  beats quoting them.
- The honest version of an argument is more defensible than the impressive one. "LightGBM alone is
  the top scorer; decision-level fusion wins on simplicity + explainability + escalation" is
  stronger than overclaiming.
- Most production ML failures are quiet — a shadowed source package, a subsample that breaks a
  sequence model, a model that decays without alarming. The work is in catching them.
